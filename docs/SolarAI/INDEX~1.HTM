Federal tax credit note (as of 2026): none of the rows above include a federal solar tax credit, and that's now accurate for every row here — the 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit (Section 25D) that applied to cash and loan purchases expired December 31, 2025 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. Homeowners who buy (self-funded, HELOC, credit union, or solar-lender loan) in 2026 get $0 in federal credit. A separate commercial credit (Section 48E) still applies through the end of 2027, but only to third-party-owned systems — i.e. solar leases and PPAs, where the leasing company (not the homeowner) claims it, typically reflected as a lower lease rate rather than a credit you receive directly. That changes the historical calculus between owning and leasing; this tool doesn't yet model lease/PPA rows (see the DIY & alternative ownership research doc for the reasoning and a plan to add one).

PV degradation: "Solar offset of usage" is the panels' effective offset in year 1, at full nameplate output. From there, effective offset shrinks by the degradation rate for every full decade elapsed — a flat, linear reduction (not compounding): at the 4% default, offset is 100% of the year-1 value for years 0-9, 96% for years 10-19, 92% for years 20-29, and so on, floored at 0% rather than going negative. This lowers the solar-reduced bill's effective offset every decade, which flows into every chart on this page that uses a solar-reduced bill: the projection chart above, and every line on the net worth chart below except "No solar" (which never had an offset to degrade). 4%/decade is a reasonable starting assumption reflecting typical panel warranties, not a guarantee for any specific system — adjust it if your installer quotes a different degradation curve.

Net worth impact by financing option

Each line shows a realistic cash trajectory over 25 years, not an abstract cost index. "Self-funded" starts holding the full system cost and spends it in one lump event; financed options never hold that lump sum at all, so their lines start near zero and ease downward with each payment instead. The two "Market investment" lines are for a homeowner who keeps that cash invested rather than spending it, so they start near the full system cost too — one skips solar, the other finances it on a HELOC while the investment keeps compounding untouched. A gray dotted "no solar" line is included for reference.

"Self-funded" tracks an actual cash balance: it starts at the full system cost (money genuinely in hand), drops to roughly zero the moment it's spent on the system, then continues to decline as ongoing solar-reduced utility bills are paid from it. Financed options never require that lump sum in the first place, so instead of a spend-down balance, their lines track the compounding opportunity cost of each payment as it's made, using the invest-return assumption above — which is why they start small and slope downward gradually rather than beginning with a cliff. The "no solar" baseline behaves the same way as the financed lines, using the full (non-offset) utility bill. These two families of lines represent different things by necessity — a lump sum you had and spent, versus a payment obligation you never needed the full amount for — so read the shapes, not just the endpoints, when comparing them.

"Market investment, no solar" and "Market investment + HELOC" are for a homeowner who has the system cost in cash but never spends it — it stays invested at the assumed return, uninterrupted, whether or not they go solar. That's why both start near the full system cost rather than at zero, unlike every line above: this cash is never drawn down and never offset by a loan, so it just compounds in the background the whole time. The first line skips solar entirely and pays the full utility bill; the second finances the system on the HELOC terms above (rate, term, and deductibility included) so the invested cash never has to move. In both, the utility bill (and loan payment, for the second) subtract at face value only, the same way "Self-funded" already treats its own utility bill above — no compounding penalty applied, since that money comes from an external pool that was never going to be invested and so isn't competing with the principal. Only the principal itself compounds, fully unencumbered, for the entire horizon. These two lines deliberately don't start on the same footing as "Self-funded" or the financed-only lines above — that's a real advantage of having cash reserves to spare, not a modeling inconsistency. The combined line currently pairs with the HELOC row specifically; swapping in credit union or solar lender terms just means calling the same underlying calculation with a different row.

Same lines, long horizon

Same lines, same math, just carried out to a 50- or 60-year horizon instead of 25, with the x-axis labeled every decade instead of every year. Figures at this range get large fast, since both the untouched invested principal and the escalating utility bill keep compounding well past where the 25-year view stops. Treat this as an illustration of how compounding behaves over a working lifetime rather than a literal 50-60 year forecast — a lot happens over that span (rate changes, moving, panel replacement) that this model has no way to account for.
