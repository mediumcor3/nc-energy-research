Federal tax credit note (as of 2026): none of the rows above include a federal solar tax credit, and that's now accurate for every row here — the 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit (Section 25D) that applied to cash and loan purchases expired December 31, 2025 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. Homeowners who buy (cash, HELOC, credit union, or solar-lender loan) in 2026 get $0 in federal credit. A separate commercial credit (Section 48E) still applies through the end of 2027, but only to third-party-owned systems — i.e. solar leases and PPAs, where the leasing company (not the homeowner) claims it, typically reflected as a lower lease rate rather than a credit you receive directly. That changes the historical calculus between owning and leasing; this tool doesn't yet model lease/PPA rows (see the DIY & alternative ownership research doc for the reasoning and a plan to add one).

Net worth impact by financing option

Each line shows a realistic cash trajectory over 25 years, not an abstract cost index. "Cash, invested" starts holding the full system cost and spends it in one lump event; financed options never hold that lump sum at all, so their lines start near zero and ease downward with each payment instead. A gray dotted "no solar" line is included for reference.

"Cash, invested" tracks an actual cash balance: it starts at the full system cost (money genuinely in hand), drops to roughly zero the moment it's spent on the system, then continues to decline as ongoing solar-reduced utility bills are paid from it. Financed options never require that lump sum in the first place, so instead of a spend-down balance, their lines track the compounding opportunity cost of each payment as it's made, using the invest-return assumption above — which is why they start small and slope downward gradually rather than beginning with a cliff. The "no solar" baseline behaves the same way as the financed lines, using the full (non-offset) utility bill. These two families of lines represent different things by necessity — a lump sum you had and spent, versus a payment obligation you never needed the full amount for — so read the shapes, not just the endpoints, when comparing them.
